Overview

In this project, we implemented a real-time realistic cloth simulator using a mass and spring based system. We built data structures to discretely represent the cloth, applied physical constraints on them, and applied numerical integration to simulate cloth movement behavior. We also implemented collision behavior (both external and self), and multiple shading techniques via the GLSL shader program. Finally, we experimented with adding multiple types of wind to simulate cloth in varying weather.

Overall, we had a ton of fun learning about how to apply physics to material movement, and exploring shading engines/techniques.

Implementation Overview: First, the pointmasses were created by looping a nested for loop, setting the y value to 1 or z value to 1.0 / (rand() % 2000 + 999) depending on orientation. Each point was also checked for pinning during this process. Then, 3 individual loops went through the points and emplace_back was used to add each of structural, shearing, and bending springs to the cloth.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration
Experiment with some the parameters in the simulation. To do so, pause the simulation at the start with P, modify the values of interest, and then resume by pressing P again. You can also restart the simulation at any time from the cloth's starting position by pressing R.
Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?

When modifying ks, it appears a low ks has much more rippling and retains energy from the dropping much better than the cloth with high ks. This difference is likely due to the fact that a higher spring constant force corrects and dampens the force of gravity, meaning the force dissipates much faster in the high ks simulation. In the low ks simulation, on the other hand, the force is retained better and ripples throughout the cloth, causing the waves and wrinkles in the cloth. Also, towards the start of the fall, the cloth with less ks appears to have its pointmasses in freefall more, most likely due to the lack of corrective force compared to the uniform "caving-in" motion of the high ks cloth. Both reach roughly the same end state, though the high ks simulation has a less noticable x-shaped diagonal across the cloth.

What about for density?

From the images above, it appears that \(density\) has a similar relationship to \(ks\), just inverted. This generally makes sense, since a hgiher density in the point masses would mean they gain more gravitational force when descending, making it harder for the dampening force from the springs to take effect and zero-out the rippling effect. The start, middle, and end states all appear to behave similarily. That said, pinned4.json is probably not the most indicative of the difference between the two. Since the only force applied is downwards, the simulations look similar. However, \(ks\) resists all types of change in the cloth, both vertical and horizontal, while \(density\) can either increase or decrease the velocity of pointmasses. For example, if the cloth was swinging, the one with higher \(ks\) would resist the swing while the one with higher \(density\) would shake more violently.

What about for damping?

In our mathematical implementation of velocity and positional updates with Verlet Integration, \(dampening\) is used to simulate the loss of energy due to heat, air resistance, etc. This means that when the \(dampening\) parameter is set to 0, the cloth never looses energy, which is noticable in how the cloth, even in the end state, still shakes around from the structural spring force. Since the energy never dissipates, it continues to shake forever. In comparison, for dampening = 1, no velocity is ever built up since it is always zero'd out, leading to a slow, roughly-linear speed fall into its end state. Also, the cloth never bounces with a perfect dampening since as soon as the upwards structural force matches the downwards gravitational force, the cloth is at equilibrium.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

Implementation Overview: Both collision types were implemented using the ray-sphere intersection equation or ray-plane intersection equation where the ray is the pm.last_position to pm.position. Intersections were detected for the sphere if the current position was within the radius as follows: abs((pm.position - origin).norm()) <= radius. For planes, the last_position and position parameters were used to check if it flipped from one side of the plane to the other: (dot(pm.last_position,normal) >= 0 && dot(pm.position,normal) <= 0) || (dot(pm.last_position,normal) <= 0 && dot(pm.position,normal) >= 0). Finally, the ray mentioned previously was used to calculate the offset position, and the frictional coefficient \(f\) was multiplied with the corrective vector.

In this simulation, \(ks\) controls the "rigidity" of the cloth or how resistant it is to the pulling force of gravity and how well it maintains its structure. Thus, at low \(ks\), the cloth bends much more, and the edges are droopier and flare out less as a result. As \(ks\) increases, the cloth becomes more resistant to the "drooping" and flares out more since the structural springs are working harder. This is especially pronounced in \(ks = 50000\), where the edges come away from the sphere much more.

Part 5: Cloth Sim
Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.

A shader program is user-defined code that a GPU uses to execute parts of the graphics pipeline. The (basic) pipeline of a shader program consists of two sequential parts: (1) the vertex shader, and (2) the fragment shader. Vertex shaders are responsible for the geometry of vertices in the rendered model. The fragment shader operates on the output of the vertex shader, using "fragments" of the rasterized model. In particular, it computes the color of rasterized fragments using the inputted vertex information and some specified shading method.

Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. Show a screenshot of your Blinn-Phong shader outputting only the ambient component, a screen shot only outputting the diffuse component, a screen shot only outputting the specular component, and one using the entire Blinn-Phong model.

The Blinn-Phong shading model is a 3-part per-vertex shading model that computes the light intensity incident according to the following equation:

where \(k_a, k_d, k_s\) are the ambient, diffuse, and specular coefficients; \(I, \ell, r = \|\ell\|\) are the intensity, ray displacement, and distance to the light source; \(n\) is the surface normal at the vertex; \(I_a\) is the ambient light intensity; \(h\) is the half-vector (i.e. bisector) of \(\ell, n\); and \(p\) is the specular power.

The first term represents ambient light, or light that would hypothetically arrive after more than one bounce. The second term represents diffuse light, which is a uniform hemispherical reflection. The final (and third) term represents specular light, approximating light reflected off of the scene in a mirror-like way.

For our shading, we used the following constant values: \(k_a = 0.2, k_d = 1, k_s = 0.8, I_a = 0.8, p = 20\).

Wind

We implemented wind in ClothSimulator.cpp by adding to the external_accelerations vector. In order to not make the wind a constant force and allow for flapping, the following random force generating code was used. 

int timer = 0; 
Vector3D* wind = new Vector3D(10,30,-50);
double windScalar = (rand() % 10 + 1);
if (windScalar <= 7 || timer > 0) {
  windScalar = 0.0;
  timer -=1;
} else {
  windScalar = 1.0/windScalar;
  timer = 100;
}
